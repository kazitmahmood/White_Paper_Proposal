\section{Intellectual Merit}

\paragraph{Challenge.}
Lithium-ion battery thermal runaway is a persistent safety risk in transportation, grid storage, and defense. Interior hot-spot temperatures can precede external warning signs by minutes to hours, yet most commercial battery management systems rely on surface temperature measurements; direct internal sensing generally requires embedded or penetrative hardware. Ultrasonic time-of-flight (TOF) has shown promise as a non-invasive thermometry channel~\cite{Cheng2024,Williams2024}, but ultrasonic features must be decoupled from state of charge, coupling condition, gas evolution, and mechanical boundary changes. Reported TOF thermometry can reach degree-scale accuracy under controlled conditions, but practical accuracy can degrade as these confounds vary. This project addresses that gap by combining nonlinear periodic-structure amplification with phase-sensitive acoustic readout.

\paragraph{Why This is New and Why Now.}
Linear ultrasonic battery thermometry exists~\cite{Cheng2024,Williams2024}, but to the best of our knowledge, nonlinear band-edge amplification of second-harmonic generation has not yet been developed as a battery temperature discriminator. The innovation is not ultrasound alone; it is converting a small temperature-induced stiffness shift into a nonlinear amplitude-ratio signal through engineered dispersion. The proposed sensing element operates near a band edge, where slow group velocity localizes energy and the second-harmonic response is expected to exhibit inverse detuning dependence. In the reduced model, harmonic balance gives $A_{2f}/A_f \sim \delta_\mathrm{NL}/(\Delta^2+\zeta^2)$, where $\Delta = f_\mathrm{drive} - f_\mathrm{edge}(T)$, $\delta_\mathrm{NL}$ is the effective nonlinear coefficient, and $\zeta$ represents damping/linewidth. Two features make this plausible for drift-resistant sensing: the band-edge frequency can be designed by unit-cell geometry, and the harmonic ratio is expected to partially cancel common-mode drive and coupling changes that corrupt absolute amplitude measurements. This project will test whether temperature-induced shifts in $f_\mathrm{edge}(T)$ produce harmonic-ratio sensitivity beyond conventional wave-speed readouts. The timing is right: nonlinear periodic-structure theory now provides a useful foundation for designing band-edge-sensitive responses~\cite{Lazarov2007}, precision CNC machining should make asymmetric Duffing-type stub geometries reproducible at the required prototype tolerance, and battery deployment creates urgent demand for interior temperature monitoring beyond surface-only BMS measurements. NewFoS targets acoustic sensing solutions for energy and transportation; this project proposes a nonlinear band-edge thermometry principle that complements current linear-acoustic approaches.

\paragraph{PI Background.}
The PI's research expertise spans nonlinear vibration, acoustics, and periodic elastic structure theory, precisely the intersection this project requires. Prior work demonstrates phase-sensitive lock-in detection of nonlinear harmonic states in piezo-driven acoustic waveguides~\cite{Mahmood2022} and a Bloch-sphere dispersion formalism for one-dimensional elastic lattices covering band-edge analysis and symmetry-governed mode hybridization~\cite{Mahmood2026a}. These contributions provide direct preparation for this project by combining harmonic balance, Floquet-Bloch dispersion, multiple-scales perturbation, and phase-sensitive acoustic measurement.


\section{Research Plan}

\paragraph{Tasks.}
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Task~1: Analytical model (months 1--4):} \emph{Problem:} A predictive model is needed that links the unit-cell Duffing coefficient, slow-wave detuning $\Delta$, damping/linewidth $\zeta$, and temperature sensitivity of $A_{2f}/A_f$ for the proposed thermometry architecture; without it the optimal drive frequency cannot be set a priori. \emph{Solution:} Harmonic balance at $f_0$ and $2f_0$ applied to a Duffing diatomic chain will derive a leading-order expression expected to scale as $A_{2f}/A_f \sim \delta_\mathrm{NL}/(\Delta^2+\zeta^2)$ under weakly nonlinear, near-band-edge assumptions. Multiple-scales analysis will bound the valid drive-amplitude range and predict a steep but bounded temperature derivative, with $\Delta^{-3}$ recovered only as a low-damping asymptote. The deliverable is a design map giving the useful detuning window, required nonlinear coefficient, damping tolerance, and predicted signal-to-drift margin for the experiment.

  \item \textbf{Task~2: FE simulation (months 3--8):} \emph{Problem:} The 1D chain model neglects plate boundary conditions, two-dimensional wave spreading, and higher-order modes that can contaminate the $2f$ channel; these effects must be resolved before fabrication. \emph{Solution:} A 30--40\,cm 6061-T6 aluminum plate waveguide (8--12 unit cells, CNC-asymmetric stubs) is implemented in a finite element environment with temperature-dependent elastic constants; a parametric sweep of stub height, width, and asymmetry ratio maps $\partial(A_{2f}/A_f)/\partial T$ while $3f$/$4f$ monitoring identifies a clean harmonic operating regime. Dispersion and mode-shape extraction verify that the selected drive excites the intended band-edge mode rather than a plate resonance artifact. Stub geometry is constrained to $\pm$0.1\,mm machining tolerance so the computational sensitivity maximum remains fabrication-relevant. A symmetric-stub FE control and a 10\% agreement target with the Task~1 reduced-order expression validate the model and fix the final geometry.

  \item \textbf{Task~3: Fabrication and experiment (months 6--12):} \emph{Problem:} Amplifier source harmonics at $2f_0$ and coupling-layer drift can mimic the structural temperature signal; genuine drift resistance must be demonstrated against controlled disturbances. \emph{Solution:} A transmit bandpass filter suppresses source harmonics; $A_{2f}/A_f$ is extracted via coherent FFT averaging over 20 tone-bursts per step as the specimen is stepped through 20--100$^\circ$C (5$^\circ$C increments) with drive voltage sinusoidally modulated $\pm$20\% to emulate coupling variation. A symmetric-stub control specimen, off-band drive point, and simultaneous linear TOF measurement separate bond, amplifier, and wave-speed effects from the engineered nonlinear response. Sensitivity (dB/$^\circ$C), repeatability, and drift-induced temperature error are reported with 95\% confidence intervals from ten repeated temperature cycles, providing a statistically robust comparison between the nonlinear waveguide and the linear TOF control.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Milestones, risk, and timeline.}
\textbf{M1 (month 4):} Reduced-order sensitivity model derived; analytical design map completed. \textbf{M2 (month 8):} Finite element sensitivity map complete; results presented at a conference. \textbf{M3 (month 12):} Plate specimen fabricated and tested; sensitivity and drift-rejection data collected; journal manuscript submitted. The primary risk (stub nonlinearity too small to produce a measurable second-harmonic ratio) is mitigated by (a)~a symmetric-stub control for common-mode cancellation, (b)~stub geometry optimization in Task~2 before machining, and (c)~input harmonic filters at the transmitter to suppress source spurious harmonics. \textbf{Timeline:} Sept--Dec~2026 analytical modeling; Jan--Apr~2027 finite element simulation and plate fabrication; May--Aug~2027 experiments, data analysis, and manuscript writing.


\section{Broader Impact}

\paragraph{Technological.}
This project aims to establish a new acoustic sensing principle in which temperature sensitivity is structurally amplified by slow-wave band-edge dynamics in a nonlinear periodic structure, rather than derived from a direct wave-speed measurement. If validated, the modality could generalize from temperature to mechanical strain, state-of-charge, and electrolyte degradation by redesigning the unit-cell geometry to shift the band edge in response to the target quantity. Successful demonstration opens follow-on funding pathways through NSF CMMI, DoE Vehicle Technologies Office, and DoD ONR, and a Year-2 effort will validate the sensor against commercial Li-ion cells under charge-discharge cycling.

\paragraph{Workforce and education.}
This project will develop the PI's expertise at the intersection of nonlinear vibration theory, periodic elastic structure design, acoustic instrumentation, and applied sensing, a distinctive combination spanning the aerospace, automotive, and energy-storage industries. Undergraduate research opportunities will be targeted at NewFoS partner Spelman College. All finite element models, analysis scripts, fabrication drawings, and raw calibration datasets will be released as open-source resources through the NewFoS community data portal.
